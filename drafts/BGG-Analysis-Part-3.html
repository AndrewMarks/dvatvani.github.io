Complexity bias in ratings?

I’ve heard many people comment on the fact that Boardgamegeek’s top 10 list appears to be dominated by really big and complex games, particularly when compared to many other curators’ top 10 lists.

There are a several interesting features in the plot above.

  1. There is a clear correlation between game rating and game complexity (average weight score). This could either mean that more complex games are generally better, or that the boardgamegeek userbase values complexity in board games.
  2. Games above the rating vs complexity trend line are more likely to be commercially successful than those beneath it. At first glance, this seems intuitive since it indicates that good games (i.e. higher rated games) sell more, but more particularly what this tells us is that the ratings bar in order for games to be successful is is set much lower for less complex games.
  3. The final interesting feature is the tail of games of low complexity and low ratings at the bottom left of the plot. This “tail of spite” consist of relatively old mass-appeal games. Every single game in the “tail of spite” was released pre-1980, with many being considerably older than that. The names of these games are shown in the table below:

Correcting for the complexity bias

We can correct for the complexity bias in the ratings to obtain a more complexity-agnostic rating of a game. We do this by determining the residuals for all games from the ratings vs weight regression that we established above. The rating residuals are effectively a measure of how much more highly a game is rated than the average game of a similar level of complexity.

BGG use a Bayesian mean to rank their games instead of taking the raw average ratings. This is designed to push games with a very low number of ratings towards the mean to prevent the top games list being dominated by highly rated games with only a handful of ratings. I’ve taken a similar approach, assuming a Bayesian prior of 0 (the average for rating residuals), with a weight of 5,000 ratings. Looking at the top 10 games using this complexity-bias correction, we find that the top games are:
